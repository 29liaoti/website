Personal Genomics Testing

Services like 23andMe offer insights into your ancestry, health risks, and genetic traits. But what are you really giving up when you share your DNA?

What is Personal Genomics Testing?

Personal genomics testing allows individuals to learn about their genetic makeup through companies like 23andMe, AncestryDNA, and MyHeritage. By sending in a saliva sample, you can discover:

  • Your ethnic background and ancestry
  • Traits like eye color, taste preferences, and more
  • Relatives you never knew existed

The industry has exploded in recent years, with millions of people sharing their most personal information: their DNA.

Why People Love It

  • Discover your ethnic heritage and ancestry
  • Connect with long-lost relatives
  • Learn about potential health risks early
  • Understand genetic traits and characteristics
  • Contribute to scientific research
  • Make informed medical decisions

The Hidden Concerns

  • Your DNA data could be hacked or stolen
  • Insurance companies might access your information
  • Law enforcement can request your genetic data
  • Family secrets may be unexpectedly revealed
  • Your relatives' privacy is also compromised
  • Data could be sold to third parties

The Science Behind Genomics Testing

The Problem Being Addressed

Many people have used a DNA testing service such as 23andMe to determine their DNA and potentially health and medical risks they might have. Although this service does test your DNA, it is not reliable and will often times mislead people as they are based off of statistical associations not a confirmed medical diagnosis. A Lot of users walk away with flase information and certainty about health conditions that they may or may not have developed. This concern has also reached the FDA and has also adressed this issue about the health related scan being inaccurate.

How the Technology Works

Step 1: Sample Collection

In order to obtain your DNA, a sample is taken from your body. Depending on the kind of test being performed, a cheek swab, blood draw, or saliva swab can be utilized for this. The cells in the sample contain all of your genetic information (Cleveland Clinic, 2023).

Step 2: DNA Extraction

When the sample arrives at the lab, researchers extract the DNA. Your genes, chromosomes, and the chemical base pairs that carry your biological information and distinguish you from other people are all found in your DNA (Cleveland Clinic, 2023).

Step 3: Laboratory Analysis

In order to find any alterations or variances in your genes and chromosomes, scientists analyze the extracted DNA. These differences are compared to established genetic patterns to see if they differ from what is normally predicted in a healthy genome (Cleveland Clinic, 2023).

Step 4: Results and Interpretation

The results are combined into a report that can tell you if you have any genetic variants associated with particular diseases or characteristics. Results indicate probability and danger rather than certainty, therefore having a variant does not always imply that you will acquire a problem (Cleveland Clinic, 2023).

Evaluating the Implications

Ancestry and Identity Implications

DNA testing has made it socially possible for everyday people to access information about their ethnic background and ancestry that was previously only available through expensive medical or genealogical research. By scanning genetic variations and comparing them against large reference databases, these services can estimate where a person's ancestors came from and connect them with biological relatives they never knew existed (Cleveland Clinic, 2023). However the results are based on statistical probability rather than confirmed fact, meaning ancestry percentages are estimates that can change as reference databases grow and improve. This raises an ethical concern about how confidently companies present these results to consumers who may not understand the difference between a probability and a certainty (World Health Organization, 2024).

Privacy and Data Security Implications

One of the most serious implications of DNA testing is what happens to your genetic data after you submit it. Unlike a password or credit card number, once it is compromised it cannot be changed or replaced. Because approximately 50% of DNA is shared with first degree relatives, one person submitting their data indirectly exposes the genetic information of their entire biological family without those relatives ever consenting. This could lead to permanent harm to others making cybersecurity around DNA databases a ethical concern.

Legal and Ethical Implications

Although there are laws that protect consumers, there are still significant protection gaps in the legal system of genetic data. The World Health Organization's 2024 ethical guidelines emphasize that consents requires individuals to fully comprehend how their data will be stored, shared, and used (World Health Organization, 2024). Nevertheless the depth of the genomic science is quickly far beyond the comprehension of most consumers leaving an ethical paradox where the companies are technically getting consent but the consumer is not really knowledgeable enough to actually give meaningful consent.
