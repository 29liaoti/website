Personal Genomics Testing

Understanding the Promise and Perils of DNA Testing Services

Services like 23andMe offer insights into your ancestry, health risks, and genetic traits. But what are you really giving up when you share your DNA?

What is Personal Genomics Testing?

Personal genomics testing allows individuals to learn about their genetic makeup through companies like 23andMe, AncestryDNA, and MyHeritage. By sending in a saliva sample, you can discover:

  • Your ethnic background and ancestry
  • Potential health risks and genetic conditions
  • Traits like eye color, taste preferences, and more
  • Relatives you never knew existed

The industry has exploded in recent years, with millions of people sharing their most personal information: their DNA.

Why People Love It

  • Discover your ethnic heritage and ancestry
  • Connect with long-lost relatives
  • Learn about potential health risks early
  • Understand genetic traits and characteristics
  • Contribute to scientific research
  • Make informed medical decisions

The Hidden Concerns

  • Your DNA data could be hacked or stolen
  • Insurance companies might access your information
  • Law enforcement can request your genetic data
  • Family secrets may be unexpectedly revealed
  • Your relatives' privacy is also compromised
  • Data could be sold to third parties

5 Biggest Risks of Sharing Your DNA

1. Data Breaches and Hacking

Genetic databases are prime targets for hackers. In 2018, MyHeritage announced that 92 million user accounts were compromised. Unlike passwords, you can't change your DNA if it's stolen.

2. Law Enforcement Access

Police have used DNA databases to solve crimes, which sounds positive until you realize your genetic data could implicate you or your relatives in investigationsâ€”even if you're innocent. The Golden State Killer case demonstrated how familial DNA can be used without consent.

3. Insurance and Employment Discrimination

While some protections exist, genetic information could potentially be used to deny coverage or employment. Companies might consider you a higher risk based on your genetic predispositions, affecting life insurance, long-term care, and disability insurance.

4. Family Secrets and Unexpected Discoveries

DNA tests have revealed unexpected paternity, unknown siblings, and family secrets that some people weren't prepared to discover. These revelations can cause emotional trauma and damage family relationships.

5. Loss of Anonymity for Your Entire Family

When you share your DNA, you're not just exposing yourselfâ€”you're revealing information about your parents, siblings, children, and extended family members who never consented to have their genetic information in a database.

WHO's New Ethical Guidelines (2024)

In November 2024, the World Health Organization released principles for ethical human genomic data collection and sharing, recognizing the growing concerns:

Individual Control: People should have meaningful control over their genomic data, including the right to withdraw consent.
Transparency: Companies must be clear about how data is used, stored, and shared with third parties.
Protection from Discrimination: Genomic data should not be used to discriminate against individuals in employment, insurance, or social settings.
Data Security: Robust cybersecurity measures must protect against breaches and unauthorized access.
Benefit Sharing: If companies profit from research using your DNA, there should be mechanisms for community benefit.

Questions to Ask Before Testing

Who owns my genetic data?

Read the fine print. Many companies claim ownership or rights to use your data for research and commercial purposes.

Can I delete my data later?

Some companies allow deletion, but it may not remove data already shared with third parties or used in research.

Who can access my information?

Understand the company's policies on sharing with researchers, pharmaceutical companies, and law enforcement.

How secure is the database?

Ask about encryption, cybersecurity measures, and their history of data breaches.

What happens if the company is sold?

Your data could be transferred to a new owner with different privacy policies and practices.
